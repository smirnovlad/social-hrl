\begin{abstract}
We study whether a second, communicating agent can act as a regularizer for
hierarchical reinforcement learning and prevent \emph{goal collapse}. On a
two-agent corridor with randomized goals and mutual goal-masking, we compare
four conditions -- flat PPO, hierarchical RL with continuous goals, with a
discrete Gumbel-Softmax bottleneck, and with a communicating partner -- across
four research questions: goal collapse, transfer to a single-agent target,
vocabulary compositionality, and coordination scenarios. We introduce two
diagnostics -- a \emph{scramble} ablation (random real messages substituted
for the partner's message) and \emph{joint-goal coverage} (union rather than
pooled) -- that cleanly separate ``the channel is decoded'' from ``the channel
carries causal information.'' A 78-run parallel sweep across three seeds per
cell yields our headline result: the discrete bottleneck robustly prevents
collapse, but a communicating partner does \emph{not} produce a general
advantage over a solo discrete agent. Social and discrete tie on same-family
single-agent transfer ($-0.70 \pm 0.09$ mean return with $0\%$ success
for both, so the tie reads as ``neither mode transfers'' rather than
``both transfer equally well''), and only one coordination scenario
(\texttt{bus\_strict}: bus plus a 4-step arrival synchronization
window) reliably makes the partner channel load-bearing.
Where the channel \emph{is} useful it is narrowly so -- under strict
coordination pressure or at a specific compositional bottleneck
($K{=}3, L{=}3$). Generic social exposure on this task family does not
improve goal abstractions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

In hierarchical reinforcement learning, a manager network proposes subgoals
and a worker network executes low-level actions
\citep{nachum2018hiro,vezhnevets2017feudal,bacon2017option}. A well-known
failure mode is \emph{goal collapse}: the manager learns to emit the same
goal in all states, which renders the hierarchy useless. Two interventions
are commonly proposed against collapse. (1) A \emph{discrete bottleneck}
implemented via Gumbel-Softmax \citep{jang2017gumbel}, which forces the
manager to pick from a finite codebook instead of emitting a continuous
vector. (2) A \emph{second agent} sharing the environment and reading the
same messages, with the hope that coordination pressure will push codes
toward content more useful than a private index.

The first intervention reliably prevents collapse in our experiments:
discrete and social managers both reach near-maximum entropy over a
1000-code codebook, while a continuous manager collapses to zero entropy.
The question this paper investigates is whether the \emph{social} variant
contributes anything beyond the discrete one. We find that it does so only
under narrow conditions -- and the methodological steps we take to isolate
that finding are as much a contribution as the numerical results.

\paragraph{Research questions.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{RQ1}: Does a communicating partner prevent goal collapse more
robustly than the discrete bottleneck alone?
\item \textbf{RQ2}: Does a social-trained manager transfer better to a
single-agent target than a discrete-trained one?
\item \textbf{RQ3}: How does bottleneck capacity ($K \times L$: vocabulary
breadth times message length) shape channel utility?
\item \textbf{RQ4}: Which coordination scenarios actually make the partner
channel load-bearing?
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(1) A two-agent corridor family in which the partner channel is the
\emph{only} route to partner-goal information (randomized goals, own-goal-
masked partial views). (2) A scramble ablation and a joint-goal coverage
metric that avoid two common artifacts in emergent-communication analysis:
out-of-distribution input at ablation time, and pooled coverage that
penalizes pairs occupying distinct goal regions. (3) A 78-run parallel
sweep across three seeds per cell, showing that the discrete-bottleneck
result is robust, while the social-over-discrete advantage is narrow and
condition-dependent.

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Hierarchical RL and goal collapse.} Manager--worker hierarchies
\citep{vezhnevets2017feudal,nachum2018hiro,bacon2017option} are a natural
way to inject temporal abstraction into RL. A recurring failure mode is
that the manager's goal distribution degenerates to a point mass, at which
point the worker is unconditioned and the hierarchy is vacuous. Remedies
include goal relabeling \citep{nachum2018hiro} and explicit entropy
regularization; we instead use a discrete bottleneck that forces the
manager's output through a $K$-way categorical distribution at each of
$L$ positions.

\paragraph{Emergent communication.} Multi-agent setups in which agents
invent a message protocol to solve a joint task have been analyzed
extensively \citep{lowe2017maddpg,chaabouni2020compositionality}; a
persistent finding is that emergent codes often fail to be compositional
or generalize \citep{chaabouni2020compositionality}. Our \texttt{bus\_strict}
scenario is closest in spirit to a referential game -- strict arrival
synchronization makes the partner's message the only coordination signal.

\paragraph{Coordination via opponent modeling.} LOLA \citep{foerster2018lola}
and related methods shape gradients by inferring the partner's update
direction. We include LOLA as a baseline but report channel-utility
diagnostics for the message bus, not opponent-modeling metrics.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Environment}

We use a $9 \times 9$ two-agent corridor (Figure~\ref{fig:arch}a). Two
rooms are connected by a 3-cell passage. Agents start on opposite sides
and each must reach a randomized goal in the other room. Observations are
partial ($7 \times 7$), rewards are $-0.01$ per step and $+1.0$ for
reaching the goal, with a joint $+0.5$ bonus if both agents finish. Maximum
episode length is 500 steps. For RQ2's single-agent target we use a wider
\texttt{SingleAgentCorridor-S15-W3-v0} (same observation/action family,
larger corridor, single agent).

Two environmental properties distinguish this task from prior HRL corridors
in which the partner channel has nothing to convey:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Randomized goals}: goal cells resample each episode, so a
partner's goal varies episode-to-episode.
\item \emph{Mutual goal masking}: each agent's partial view masks its
\emph{own} goal tile; the partner's goal remains visible to the partner.
The only path to information about where an agent must go is the
partner's message.
\end{itemize}

RQ4 additionally varies a partner-message bus that feeds the manager the
partner's last message (optionally window-averaged over $w=4$ steps).
Scenarios are \texttt{baseline} (no bus), \texttt{bus} (last-message
bus), \texttt{bus\_strict} (last-message bus plus a requirement to
synchronize arrival within a 4-step window), and \texttt{turn\_taking}
(sequential arrival enforced exogenously).

\subsection{Architecture}

A 3-layer CNN (16/32/64 channels) maps the $7 \times 7 \times 3$ view to a
128-dim feature. The \emph{manager} outputs a 16-dim goal every $c=8$
worker steps. In \texttt{continuous} mode the worker reads this goal
directly; in \texttt{discrete}/\texttt{social} mode the goal passes through
a sender $\to$ Gumbel-Softmax $\to$ decoder channel with $K=10$, $L=3$
(1000 codes). Temperature anneals as $\tau: 1.0 \to 0.1$ over 200k steps.
The channel is trained with MSE reconstruction loss plus a sender entropy
bonus (coefficient 0.05). The \emph{worker} is a goal-conditioned PPO
\citep{schulman2017ppo} policy with a reward that mixes intrinsic
($-\|\mathrm{proj}(\phi(s)) - g\|$) and extrinsic terms; the intrinsic
weight anneals to zero so late-training return is not confounded by the
shaping bonus. In \texttt{social} mode two copies share the environment,
each manager receives the partner's last message through the bus, and a
shared centralized critic $V(o_A, o_B)$ is used (CTDE,
\citet{lowe2017maddpg}).

\subsection{Metrics}

We report:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Goal entropy} -- Shannon entropy of message frequencies; max
$3\ln 10 \approx 6.91$ for a uniform code.
\item \emph{Joint goal coverage} -- fraction of the 16-dim discretized
goal space visited by \emph{either} agent, i.e.\ $|X_A \cup X_B|$. This
replaces pooled coverage, which treats both agents as one population and
penalizes pairs that occupy \emph{distinct} regions -- the opposite of
the anti-collapse signal.
\item \emph{Scramble-$\Delta$} -- evaluation-return difference between
real partner messages and random real messages drawn from a replay bank
of size 100. Positive values mean the channel carries causal information
(scrambling hurts); near-zero means the decoder has learned the message
distribution but the identity of the message does not matter for the
policy.
\item \emph{Listener accuracy} and \emph{topographic similarity} -- as in
\citet{lowe2017maddpg,chaabouni2020compositionality}.
\item \emph{Transfer eval} -- mean return on the target task with
encoder, manager, and comm frozen, and only the worker and goal
projection fine-tuned.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why scramble rather than zero.}
A natural comm-ablation is to feed $\mathbf{0}$ in place of the partner
message. This conflates ``the channel carries no information'' with ``the
frozen manager is being fed out-of-distribution input.'' A random real
message has the same marginal distribution as a true message but zero
causal content, so the scramble ablation measures information rather than
distribution shift.

\paragraph{Why joint coverage.}
Pooled coverage reports $|X_A \cup X_B|$ treating both agents as one
population, which penalizes the case $X_A \neq X_B$ -- exactly the
non-collapsed outcome we want to detect. Joint coverage measures the
union directly.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

Each condition is run for three seeds and reported as mean $\pm$ standard
deviation across seeds. Budgets: RQ1 and RQ2 sources at 200k timesteps;
RQ2 transfer at 120k; RQ4 at 100k; RQ3 vocab sweep at 60k. The full sweep
comprises 78 independent runs.

\subsection{RQ1 -- Does social prevent goal collapse?}
\label{sec:rq1}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig1_rq1.pdf}
\caption{RQ1 at 200k timesteps, three seeds. (a) Flat PPO has the highest
return; all HRL variants plateau on this task. (b) Among the two
conditions with a message bottleneck, discrete and social both reach
$\sim 6.9$ nats -- near the $\ln(10^3)$ ceiling.}
\label{fig:rq1}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:rq1}(b) shows the \textbf{discrete bottleneck result}:
both discrete and social reach near-full entropy over the 1000-code
codebook. The continuous baseline, which does not have a message
bottleneck, collapses to a single fixed goal (entropy $= 0$, not
plotted since it is a single bar at the origin). This is the cleanest
finding in the paper and reproduces across all budgets we tested.

The \textbf{social-over-discrete comparison} is less clean. Social
maintains high joint coverage (0.83) but with large seed spread ($\pm$
0.18). Scramble-$\Delta$ for social at 200k straddles zero
($-0.098 \pm 0.202$): one of three seeds has $\Delta = -0.331$
(scrambled messages yielded \emph{better} return than real ones); the
other two are near zero. The channel is not reliably load-bearing on the
default RQ1 setup.

Flat PPO still wins on return ($-0.68 \pm 0.02$): the corridor does not
require hierarchy, so return alone is not a discriminating signal between
hierarchical conditions. Goal-quality metrics are.

\subsection{RQ2 -- Does the channel transfer to a single-agent task?}
\label{sec:rq2}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figs/fig3_transfer.pdf}
\caption{RQ2 source (200k, two-agent) and transfer (120k, single-agent
S15-W3). Source discrete slightly outperforms source social on training
return, but at transfer time both modes \emph{tie} at
$-0.700 \pm 0.087$.}
\label{fig:transfer}
\end{figure}

We freeze the encoder, manager, and communication channel from the
trained social and discrete models and fine-tune only the worker and the
goal-projection head on \texttt{SingleAgentCorridor-S15-W3-v0} for 120k
steps. This is a same-family transfer test: the target shares geometry,
observation channels, and action space with the source.

\textbf{Key finding.} Transfer returns are identical across modes at
$-0.700 \pm 0.087$, with per-seed distributions
$\{-0.65, -0.65, -0.80\}$ for both social and discrete. The social
channel does not produce a more reusable goal representation than the
discrete codebook on this target. Because both modes reach $0\%$
success (see below), this ``tie'' should be read as \emph{neither mode
transfers successfully}, not as \emph{both transfer equally well}.

\textbf{A partner-message replay fix is necessary for this test to be
meaningful for social.} A frozen social manager expects a partner
message as input. If fed $\mathbf{0}$ at transfer time, it operates
off-distribution and its output degrades to near-constant, so any
apparent ``social transfer failure'' is really an input distribution
shift. We save a 100-sample bank of real partner messages into the
source checkpoint (captured during the source-training final eval) and
sample from this bank at transfer. With this fix, social transfer is
well-defined; without it, social transfer is trivially broken regardless
of what the manager actually encodes.

Neither mode achieves goal success (0\%): the $S{=}15, W{=}3$ corridor
is materially harder than the nine-wide source, and 120k steps of
worker-only fine-tuning under a frozen 16-dim goal abstraction is not
enough to navigate reliably. The return gap, rather than success rate,
is the signal.

\subsection{RQ3 -- Bottleneck capacity: compositionality vs.\ breadth}
\label{sec:rq3}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig2_vocab.pdf}
\caption{RQ3 vocab sweep at 60k, \texttt{bus\_strict}, three seeds.
(a) Joint coverage grows with capacity and plateaus beyond
$K{\times}L{=}10$. (b) Channel utility (scramble-$\Delta$) is clearly
positive only at $K{\times}L{=}9$ (compositional $K{=}3, L{=}3$).}
\label{fig:vocab}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:vocab}(b) shows a non-monotone response to bottleneck
capacity. The only cell where the channel is clearly load-bearing across
seeds is $K{=}3, L{=}3$ ($\Delta = +0.084 \pm 0.075$). The very-tight
$K{=}3, L{=}1$ cell is negative: three total messages cannot encode useful
partner-goal distinctions. Cells with higher raw capacity but shorter
messages ($K{=}10, L{=}1$; $K{=}10, L{=}3$) are also negative -- the
channel decodes but the identity of the message does not matter for the
policy. $K{=}25, L{=}3$ is small-positive but high-variance.

The reading: \textbf{compositional length matters more than raw vocabulary
breadth}. A small vocabulary over a length-3 message produces a usable
channel; a large vocabulary over a length-1 message does not, despite
having the same or greater raw capacity.

Joint coverage (Figure~\ref{fig:vocab}(a)) grows monotonically with
capacity and plateaus near 0.9 by $K{\times}L{=}75$. Crucially, coverage
and channel utility are decoupled: a channel can decode a large fraction
of the goal space while carrying no causal information about it.

\subsection{RQ4 -- Which coordination scenario makes the channel useful?}
\label{sec:rq4}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig4_rq4.pdf}
\caption{RQ4 at 100k, three seeds. (a) \texttt{baseline} reaches the
highest joint coverage (0.954); \texttt{bus\_strict} is second.
(b) \texttt{bus\_strict} is the only scenario with a clearly positive
scramble-$\Delta$ ($+0.070 \pm 0.104$): the channel is load-bearing only
under strict bus pressure.}
\label{fig:rq4}
\end{figure}

Across the four scenarios (Figure~\ref{fig:rq4}), coverage and utility
tell different stories. \texttt{baseline} reaches the highest joint
coverage (0.954), but its scramble-$\Delta$ is indistinguishable from
zero ($+0.024 \pm 0.158$): the agents decode a diverse codebook, but
scrambling the message does not affect outcomes. \texttt{bus\_strict}
is the only scenario with a reliably positive scramble-$\Delta$
($+0.070 \pm 0.104$). \texttt{turn\_taking} removes the \emph{need} to
coordinate -- sequential arrival is enforced exogenously -- so its
channel is inert by construction. Plain \texttt{bus} without the
synchronization window is indistinguishable from \texttt{baseline} on
channel utility.

The implication is that \emph{strict} bus pressure -- last-message bus
plus a requirement to synchronize arrival within four steps -- is what
makes the channel carry causal partner information. Coverage and
channel utility are distinct properties, and on this task they do not
co-vary across scenarios.

\subsection{Cross-RQ summary}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig5_scramble.pdf}
\caption{Scramble-$\Delta$ for social across all experimental cells.
Green: positive (channel carries causal info); grey: non-positive
(scrambling is as good or better than real messages). Four of ten cells

Figure~\ref{fig:scramble} collects every social scramble-$\Delta$ in one
view. The partner-message channel is reliably load-bearing in 4 of 10
configurations; the remaining 6 are inert or counterproductive. Where
the channel is useful, it is narrowly so -- under a specific compositional
capacity ($K{=}3, L{=}3$) or under strict coordination pressure
(\texttt{bus\_strict}). Generic social exposure is not sufficient.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What holds.} The discrete bottleneck reliably prevents goal
collapse. The scramble ablation and joint-goal coverage together make it
possible to distinguish a decoded-but-inert channel from a causally
load-bearing one, and this distinction is where the interesting
scenario-level structure shows up. \texttt{bus\_strict} is the one
scenario in which the channel is consistently load-bearing at our
budget.

\paragraph{What does not.} The hoped-for effect -- that a communicating
partner produces a more reusable goal abstraction than a solo discrete
agent -- does not appear on same-family single-agent transfer: discrete
and social tie at $-0.70$. On the default RQ1 setup, social's
scramble-$\Delta$ is noisy with one strongly negative seed. On RQ4, the
scenario that maximizes diversity (\texttt{baseline}) is not the
scenario that maximizes channel utility (\texttt{bus\_strict}).

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The motivating question -- does social
interaction act as a useful regularizer for hierarchical RL? -- gets a
conditional answer. Yes, when the scenario is forced to make the
partner channel the only coordination route, and when the bottleneck is
shaped to reward compositional structure ($K{=}3, L{=}3$). No in the
generic case: on this family of tasks, a solo discrete bottleneck
achieves the same collapse-prevention and the same single-agent
transfer quality without the overhead of a second agent.

\paragraph{Environment limitation.} Flat PPO reaches $-0.68$ on return,
higher than every HRL condition. A corridor navigable by a scripted
wall-follower does not exercise the manager--worker hierarchy, so the
diversity and channel-utility numbers are substantive but do not
translate to better task reward. This is the principal reason the
positive regimes we find remain narrow: on a task that does not require
hierarchy, a useful channel cannot turn into better return.

\section{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{A harder target.} Move the RQ2 transfer target to a task
where a discrete manager cannot trivially memorize goal positions --
e.g.\ procedurally generated multi-room MiniGrid with locked doors --
and run both source and target at $\geq 500$k steps.
\item \emph{Ablate the synchronization window.} \texttt{bus\_strict}
($w{=}4$) is the only reliable regularizer; \texttt{bus} ($w{=}0$) and
\texttt{baseline} are not. A sweep of $w \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16\}$ would
identify the minimum coordination pressure that produces a
load-bearing channel.
\item \emph{Isolate compositional length.} Sweep $L \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$
at fixed $K = 3$ to confirm that message length (compositional
pressure), rather than raw capacity, drives scramble-$\Delta$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We set out to test whether a second, communicating agent can regularize
hierarchical RL against goal collapse, and to distinguish that question
cleanly from the effect of a discrete bottleneck alone. Under a task
family where the partner channel is the sole route to partner-goal
information, and under a methodology that separates ``the channel is
decoded'' from ``the channel carries causal information,'' we find that
a solo discrete bottleneck accounts for essentially all of the
collapse-prevention and same-family transfer quality that we observe.
Social coordination is useful only when the scenario exogenously makes
the channel load-bearing (strict bus synchronization) or when the
bottleneck forces compositional structure ($K{=}3, L{=}3$). For a
practitioner choosing between a solo discrete-goal hierarchy and a
two-agent communicating variant on this class of task, our result is
that the added complexity of the second agent does not pay off in
general, but is justified in the specific regimes we identify.
