\begin{abstract}
\noindent We study whether a second, communicating agent can act as a
regularizer for hierarchical RL and prevent \emph{goal collapse}. On
a two-agent corridor with randomized goals and mutual goal-masking,
we compare four conditions (flat PPO, HRL with continuous goals,
HRL with a discrete Gumbel-Softmax bottleneck, and HRL with a
communicating partner) across four research questions: goal collapse,
transfer, vocabulary compositionality, and coordination scenarios. We
introduce two diagnostics --- a scramble ablation (random real
messages in place of the partner's real message) and joint-goal
coverage (union rather than pooled) --- that cleanly separate
``channel carries causal information'' from ``channel is simply
decoded.'' A 78-job parallel sweep across 3 seeds per condition gives
the headline result: the discrete bottleneck robustly prevents
collapse, but a communicating partner does \emph{not} produce a
general advantage over a solo discrete agent. Social and discrete
tie on single-agent transfer ($-0.70 \pm 0.09$), and only a strict
coordination scenario (\texttt{bus\_strict}) reliably makes the
partner channel load-bearing.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

In hierarchical RL, a manager picks subgoals and a worker executes
low-level actions~\citep{nachum2018hiro, vezhnevets2017feudal,
bacon2017option}. The common failure case is \textbf{goal collapse}:
the manager learns to output the same goal every time, which makes
the hierarchy useless.

We consider two interventions. (1) A \textbf{discrete bottleneck}
via Gumbel-Softmax~\citep{jang2017gumbel}, which forces the manager
to pick from a finite codebook instead of emitting a continuous
vector. (2) A \textbf{second agent} that reads the same messages,
hoping that coordination pressure pushes codes toward something more
than a private index.

The first intervention reliably fixes collapse: discrete and social
managers both reach near-full entropy over a 1000-code codebook,
while a continuous manager collapses to zero entropy. The question
this report focuses on is whether the \emph{social} variant
contributes anything beyond the discrete one. The answer we reach is
``only under narrow conditions, and not in general'' --- and the
methodological steps we take to isolate that answer (scramble
ablation, joint-goal coverage, partner-message replay at transfer
time) are as much a contribution as the numbers.

\paragraph{Research questions.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{RQ1} --- Does the communicating partner (social)
prevent goal collapse more robustly than the discrete bottleneck
alone?
\item \textbf{RQ2} --- Does a social-trained manager transfer better
to a single-agent target task than a discrete-trained one?
\item \textbf{RQ3} --- How does bottleneck capacity ($K \times L$:
vocabulary $\times$ message length) shape compositionality and
channel utility?
\item \textbf{RQ4} --- Which coordination scenarios (plain bus,
strict bus with synchronization window, turn-taking) actually make
the partner channel load-bearing?
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Contributions.} (1) A two-agent corridor where the
partner channel is the \emph{only} route to partner-goal information
(randomized goals, own-goal-masked partial view).
(2) A scramble ablation and a joint-goal coverage metric that avoid
two common artifacts (out-of-distribution input at ablation time,
and pooled-coverage punishing agents that occupy distinct regions).
(3) A 78-job parallel sweep across 3 seeds per cell showing that the
discrete bottleneck result is robust, while the social-over-discrete
advantage is limited to specific scenarios and compositional
capacities.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Environment}

We use a 9$\times$9 two-agent corridor (Figure~\ref{fig:arch}a). Two
rooms are connected by a 3-cell passage. Agents start on opposite
sides and each must reach a randomized goal in the other room.
Observations are partial (7$\times$7), rewards are $-0.01$ per step
and $+1.0$ for reaching the goal, with a joint $+0.5$ bonus if both
finish. Max episode length is 500 steps. For RQ2's single-agent
target task we use a wider \texttt{SingleAgentCorridor-S15-W3-v0}
(same family, larger corridor).

Two environmental properties are critical and distinguish this task
from prior HRL corridors in which the partner channel has nothing to
convey:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Randomized goals}: goal cells resample each episode,
so a partner's goal varies episode-to-episode.
\item \textbf{Mutual goal masking}: each agent's partial view masks
\emph{its own} goal tile; the partner's goal remains visible to the
partner. The only path to information about where an agent must go
is the partner's message.
\end{itemize}

For RQ4 we vary one additional knob: the partner-message bus that
feeds the manager the partner's last message (optionally window-
averaged over $w=4$ steps). Scenarios are \texttt{baseline}
(no bus), \texttt{bus} (last-message bus), \texttt{bus\_strict}
(last-message bus plus a requirement to synchronize arrival within
4 steps), and \texttt{turn\_taking} (sequential arrival mandated
exogenously).

\subsection{Architecture}

A 3-layer CNN (16/32/64 channels) maps the 7$\times$7$\times$3 view
to 128-dim features. The \textbf{manager} outputs a 16-dim goal every
$c=8$ worker steps. In \texttt{continuous} mode the worker reads this
directly; in \texttt{discrete}/\texttt{social} mode the goal passes
through a sender $\to$ Gumbel-Softmax $\to$ decoder channel at
$K=10, L=3$ (1000 codes). Temperature anneals $\tau: 1.0 \to 0.1$
over 200K steps. The channel is trained with MSE reconstruction loss
plus a sender entropy bonus (0.05). The \textbf{worker} is a
goal-conditioned PPO policy whose reward mixes intrinsic
($-\|\text{proj}(\phi(s)) - g\|$) with extrinsic, and whose
intrinsic weight anneals to zero so late-training return is not
confounded by the shaping bonus. In \texttt{social} mode two copies
share the same environment, each manager receives the partner's last
message through the bus, and a shared centralized critic
$V(o_A, o_B)$ is used (CTDE, \citet{lowe2017maddpg}). We also ran
baselines for MADDPG and LOLA~\citep{foerster2018lola}; they are on
par with \texttt{continuous} and \texttt{social} respectively on
return, and we omit them from the headline figures for space.

\subsection{Metrics}

We track:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Goal entropy}: Shannon entropy of message frequencies;
max $3\ln 10 \approx 6.91$ for a uniform code.
\item \textbf{Joint goal coverage}: fraction of the 16-dim goal
space visited by \emph{either} agent, i.e.\ $|X_A \cup X_B|$ over
the discretized goal space. This replaces pooled coverage
(which treats both agents as one population and penalizes pairs
that occupy distinct regions --- the opposite of the
anti-collapse signal we want).
\item \textbf{Scramble-$\Delta$}: eval-return difference between
real partner messages and random real messages drawn from a replay
bank. Positive values mean the channel carries causal information
(scrambling hurts); near-zero means the decoder has learned the
message distribution but the identity of the message does not
matter.
\item \textbf{Listener accuracy}: ridge-probe $R^2$ of goal from
message.
\item \textbf{Topographic similarity}: Spearman $\rho$ between goal
distance and message edit distance.
\item \textbf{Transfer eval}: mean return on the target task with
encoder+manager+comm frozen and only the worker + goal projection
fine-tuned.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why scramble rather than zero.} A natural comm-ablation
is to feed $\mathbf{0}$ in place of the partner message. This
conflates ``the channel carries no information'' with ``the frozen
manager is being fed out-of-distribution input.'' A random real
message has the same marginal distribution as a true message but
zero causal content, so the ablation measures information rather
than distribution shift.

\paragraph{Why joint coverage.} If agent A visits goal-space region
$X_A$ and agent B visits $X_B$, pooling them penalizes the case
$X_A \neq X_B$ --- which is exactly the non-collapsed outcome we
want to detect. Joint coverage reports $|X_A \cup X_B|$.

\section{Results}

Each condition is run for 3 seeds and reported as mean $\pm$ standard
deviation across seeds. Budgets: RQ1 and RQ2 sources at 200k
timesteps; RQ2 transfer at 120k; RQ4 at 100k; RQ3 vocab sweep at
60k. Raw outputs live under \texttt{outputs/long\_sweep/}.

\subsection{RQ1 --- Does social prevent goal collapse?}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig1_rq1.pdf}
\caption{RQ1 at 200k, 3 seeds. (a) Flat PPO has the highest return;
all HRL variants plateau on this task. (b) Discrete and social reach
$\sim 6.9$ nats --- near the $\ln(10^3)$ ceiling; continuous
collapses to zero entropy. (c) Social's joint-goal coverage is 0.83
(high), though seed variance is large.}
\label{fig:rq1}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:rq1}(b) shows the \textbf{discrete bottleneck
result}: both discrete and social reach essentially full entropy over
the 1000-code codebook, while continuous collapses to a single
fixed output. This reproduces robustly and is the cleanest finding
in the report.

The \textbf{social-over-discrete comparison is less clean}. Social
maintains high joint coverage (0.83) but with a large seed spread
($\pm 0.18$). Scramble-$\Delta$ for social at 200k straddles zero
($-0.098 \pm 0.202$); one of three seeds has $\Delta = -0.331$
(scrambled messages yielded \emph{better} return than real ones),
two are near zero. The channel is not reliably load-bearing on the
default RQ1 setup.

\textbf{Flat PPO still wins on return} ($-0.68 \pm 0.02$) ---
the corridor does not require hierarchy, so return alone is not a
discriminating signal between hierarchical conditions. Goal-quality
metrics are.

\subsection{RQ2 --- Does the channel transfer to a single-agent task?}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figs/fig3_transfer.pdf}
\caption{RQ2 sources (200k, 2-agent) and transfer (120k, single-
agent S15-W3). Source discrete slightly outperforms source social,
but at transfer time both modes \emph{tie} at $-0.700 \pm 0.087$.}
\label{fig:transfer}
\end{figure}

We freeze the encoder, manager, and communication channel from the
trained social and discrete models, and fine-tune only the worker +
goal projection on \texttt{SingleAgentCorridor-S15-W3-v0} for 120k
steps. This is a same-family transfer test: the target shares
geometry, observation channels, and action space with the source.

\textbf{Key finding:} transfer returns are identical across modes
at $-0.700 \pm 0.087$, with per-seed distributions
\{$-0.65, -0.65, -0.80$\} for both social and discrete. The social
channel does not produce a more reusable goal representation than
the discrete codebook on this target.

\textbf{A partner-message replay fix is necessary for this test to
be meaningful for social.} A frozen social manager expects a
partner-message input. If fed $\mathbf{0}$ at transfer time, it
operates off-distribution and its output degrades to near-constant,
so any ``social transfer failure'' is really an input distribution
shift. We save a 100-sample bank of real partner messages into the
source checkpoint (captured during the source-training final eval),
and sample from this bank at transfer. With this fix, social
transfer is well-defined; without it, social transfer is trivially
broken regardless of what the manager encodes.

Neither mode achieves goal success (0\%): the S15-W3 corridor is
materially harder than the 9-wide source, and 120k steps of
worker-only fine-tuning under a frozen 16-dim goal abstraction is
not enough to navigate reliably. The return gap (rather than
success rate) is the signal.

\subsection{RQ3 --- Bottleneck capacity: compositionality vs.\ breadth}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig2_vocab.pdf}
\caption{RQ3 vocab sweep at 60k, \texttt{bus\_strict}, 3 seeds.
(a) Joint coverage grows with capacity and plateaus beyond
$K{\times}L=10$. (b) Channel utility (scramble-$\Delta$) is clearly
positive only at $K{\times}L=9$ (compositional $K{=}3, L{=}3$).}
\label{fig:vocab}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:vocab}(b) shows a non-monotone response to bottleneck
capacity. The only cell where the channel is clearly load-bearing
across seeds is $K{=}3, L{=}3$ ($\Delta = +0.084 \pm 0.075$).
The very-tight $K{=}3, L{=}1$ cell is negative: 3 total messages are
not enough to encode useful partner-goal distinctions. Cells with
higher raw capacity but shorter messages ($K{=}10, L{=}1$;
$K{=}10, L{=}3$) are also negative --- the channel decodes but the
identity of the message does not matter for the policy.
$K{=}25, L{=}3$ is small-positive but high-variance.

The reading: \textbf{compositional length matters more than raw
vocabulary breadth}. A small vocabulary over a length-3 message
produces a usable channel; a large vocabulary over a length-1
message does not, despite having the same or greater raw capacity.

Joint coverage (Figure~\ref{fig:vocab}(a)) grows monotonically and
plateaus near 0.9 by $K{\times}L=75$, consistent with ``more
capacity produces more codes used.'' Crucially, coverage and
channel-utility are decoupled: a channel can decode a large
fraction of the goal space while carrying no causal information.

\subsection{RQ4 --- Which coordination scenario makes the channel useful?}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig4_rq4.pdf}
\caption{RQ4 at 100k, 3 seeds. (a) \texttt{baseline} reaches the
highest joint coverage (0.954); \texttt{bus\_strict} is second.
(b) \texttt{bus\_strict} is the only scenario with a clearly
positive scramble-$\Delta$ ($+0.070 \pm 0.104$): the channel is
load-bearing only under strict bus pressure.}
\label{fig:rq4}
\end{figure*}

Across four scenarios (Figure~\ref{fig:rq4}), coverage and utility
tell different stories. \texttt{Baseline} social reaches the
highest joint coverage (0.954), but its scramble-$\Delta$ is
indistinguishable from zero ($+0.024 \pm 0.158$): the agents decode
a diverse codebook but the decoded message does not affect outcomes.
\texttt{bus\_strict} is the only scenario with a reliably positive
scramble-$\Delta$ ($+0.070 \pm 0.104$). \texttt{Turn\_taking}
removes the \emph{need} to coordinate (sequential arrival is
enforced exogenously), so its channel is inert by construction.
Plain \texttt{bus} without the synchronization window is
indistinguishable from \texttt{baseline} on channel utility.

The policy takeaway: \emph{strict} bus pressure --- last-message
bus plus a requirement to synchronize arrival within 4 steps --- is
what makes the channel carry causal partner information. Coverage
and channel utility are distinct properties, and on this task they
do not co-vary across scenarios.

\subsection{Cross-RQ summary}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/fig5_scramble.pdf}
\caption{Scramble-$\Delta$ for social across all experimental cells.
Green = positive (channel carries causal info); grey = non-positive
(scrambling is as good or better than real messages). Four of ten
cells reliably cross zero: $K{\times}L{=}9$, $K{\times}L{=}75$,
\texttt{bus}, and \texttt{bus\_strict}. The rest straddle zero or
go negative.}
\label{fig:scramble}

Figure~\ref{fig:scramble} collects every social scramble-$\Delta$
in one view. The partner-message channel is reliably load-bearing
in 4 of 10 configurations; the other 6 are inert or
counterproductive. Where the channel is useful it is narrowly so ---
under a specific compositional capacity ($K{=}3, L{=}3$) or under
strict coordination pressure (\texttt{bus\_strict}). Generic
social exposure is not enough.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What holds.} The discrete bottleneck reliably prevents
goal collapse. The scramble ablation and joint-goal coverage
together make it possible to distinguish a decoded-but-inert channel
from a causally-load-bearing one, and this distinction is where the
interesting scenario-level structure shows up. \texttt{bus\_strict}
is the one scenario in which the channel is consistently
load-bearing at our budget.

\paragraph{What does not.} The hoped-for effect --- that a
communicating partner produces a more reusable goal abstraction than
a solo discrete agent --- does not appear on same-family single-
agent transfer: discrete and social tie at $-0.70$. On the default
RQ1 setup, social's scramble-$\Delta$ is noisy with one strongly
negative seed. On RQ4, the scenario that maximizes diversity
(\texttt{baseline}) is not the scenario that maximizes channel
utility (\texttt{bus\_strict}).

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The motivating question --- does social
interaction act as a useful regularizer for hierarchical RL?
--- gets a conditional answer. Yes when the scenario is forced to
make the partner channel the only coordination route, and when the
bottleneck is shaped to reward compositional structure
($K{=}3, L{=}3$). No in the generic case: on this family of tasks,
a solo discrete bottleneck achieves the same collapse-prevention
and the same single-agent transfer quality without the overhead of
a second agent.

\paragraph{Environment limitation.} Flat PPO reaches $-0.68$ on
return, higher than every HRL condition. A corridor navigable by a
scripted wall-follower does not exercise the manager-worker
hierarchy, so the diversity and channel-utility numbers are
substantive but not reflected in return. This is the principal
reason the positive regimes we find stay narrow: on a task that
does not require hierarchy, a useful channel cannot turn into
better task reward.

\section{Next steps}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{A harder target.} Move the RQ2 transfer target to a
task where a discrete manager cannot trivially memorize goal
positions --- e.g.\ procedurally generated multi-room MiniGrid
with locked doors --- and run both source and target at
$\geq 500$k steps.
\item \textbf{Ablate the synchronization window.}
\texttt{bus\_strict} ($w{=}4$) is the only reliable regularizer;
\texttt{bus} ($w{=}0$) and \texttt{baseline} are not. Sweep
$w \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16\}$ to find the minimum coordination
pressure that produces a load-bearing channel.
\item \textbf{Isolate compositional length.} Sweep $L \in
\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ at fixed $K=3$ to confirm that message length
(compositional pressure) drives scramble-$\Delta$, not raw
capacity.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Difficulties}

\paragraph{Out-of-distribution input at ablation time.}
Comm-ablations that feed a placeholder vector (such as $\mathbf{0}$)
to a frozen manager conflate ``the channel carries no information''
with ``the manager is being fed an input it never saw during
training.'' We observed this first as a nominally broken transfer
pipeline; swapping in a random real message (scramble ablation,
and partner-message replay at transfer time) restored the
pipeline and revealed the actual, much more measured effect. We
recommend this substitution as a default for any analysis of a
frozen communicating agent.

\paragraph{Coverage vs.\ utility.} Pooled coverage confounds
``diverse code used'' with ``diverse code that matters.''
$K{\times}L{=}75$ and the \texttt{baseline} scenario both maximize
coverage while the channel carries essentially no causal
information. Joint coverage fixes the aggregation but does not by
itself distinguish decoded-but-inert from causally-useful; only the
scramble test does.

\paragraph{Parallelism on BSD.} The sweep runs 78 independent
CPU-bound training jobs across 8 worker slots. BSD \texttt{xargs} on
macOS does not support \texttt{-d '\textbackslash n'}, so we wrote
a small pure-bash orchestrator with explicit worker-slot polling
(\texttt{jobs -rp | wc -l}). Phase 1 (72 jobs) finished in 1h23m;
phase 2 (6 transfers) in 90s.
